Word Cloud

This word cloud was generated from the "caption" field of the dataset, following our extensive data engineering and analysis with Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques. It effectively highlights the most prevalent words found in eyewitness accounts of Bigfoot encounters.

  • The frequent occurrence of the word "like" is particularly notable. Upon closer examination, we deduced that it often reflects a degree of uncertainty in the witnesses' accounts, betraying a subconscious hesitancy about their experiences. Typically, it is used in contexts such as "animal-like creature" (for instance, "I saw a creature resembling a bear darting before me").
  • The words "saw" and "heard" are also prominent. This suggests that eyewitness accounts and auditory experiences play a crucial role in these sightings. However, there is an undercurrent of doubt inherent in these personal narratives.

Bar Chart Race

The Bar Chart Race visualizes the temporal distribution of sighting counts, utilizing data from the "year" and "state" columns after distilling essential information from the textual descriptions. It illustrates the historical trends of reports in the U.S., highlighting notable periods.

  • In 1949, we observe a significant uptick as more states begin to report sightings.
  • The year 1960 marks a rapid escalation in reports, showcasing a remarkable acceleration in the number of encounters.
  • Throughout the recorded history of these reports, "Washington”, “Ohio”, and “California" consistently emerge as the top three states with the highest frequency of sightings, probably caused by geographical and cultural influence.

Line Chart Witness Count

The Line Chart, drawing from the "witness count" column of our dataset, corroborates the earlier analysis, pinpointing 1960 as a watershed year when reports of Bigfoot sightings surged dramatically.
This chart elucidates two most prominent peaks in reporting—the years 1980 and 2000. While there is a discernible downward trend post-2000, the volume of reports remains substantial, which may be attributed to the ease of remote communication in modern times, coupled with the enduring popularity of Bigfoot folklore.

Bubble Map

The Bubble Map visualizes the geographic distribution of Bigfoot sightings, drawing from the "Longitude" and "Latitude" fields in our dataset. Each bubble's size is determined by the number of sightings in that area, and bubbles are optionally clustered together based on proximity and density, adjustable via user controls for "Epsilon" and "MinPts". The map is designed to be interactive, allowing users to adjust clustering settings and instantly see changes in the visualization offering a spatial analysis of these events.
This visualization aids in understanding the geographical spread and cultural penetration of Bigfoot sightings across the United States.

  • In regions such as the Pacific Northwest and the Great Lakes area, the bubbles are significantly larger, indicating a higher frequency of reported sightings.
  • This phenomenon may be influenced by the dense forest cover and associated folklore prevalent in these regions.
  • The map also highlights smaller, yet notable concentrations of sightings in areas like Florida and Texas, suggesting that Bigfoot legends are not confined to colder, forested regions alone.

Scatterplot with Shapes

The Scatterplot visualizes Bigfoot sightings data, employing attributes "State", "Confusion", "Supernatural" , and "Count_Bigfoot_sightings" to enrich the analysis. Each point's position on the x-axis corresponds to the state where the sighting occurred, while the y-axis represents the count of sightings reported in that state.

Points are color-coded based on the "Confusion" level associated with each sighting, illustrating the clarity of the witness accounts. Additionally, the shape of each point differs according to the "Supernatural" belief level, with circles representing "Low" and triangles for "High" supernatural elements reported, providing insight into the psychological context of the sightings. The scatterplot is equipped with interactive filtering enabling users to explore patterns and correlations.

  • States like Washington and California, known for their rich folklore, show a higher frequency of sightings with varied confusion and supernatural levels, indicating cultural influences on the reports.
  • Conversely, sightings in more urbanized states like New York and New Jersey show lower overall sightings but with higher confusion levels, possibly reflecting urban myths or misidentifications.
  • The visualization reveals that states with the highest sighting counts often exhibit very high confusion and high supernatural belief levels among witnesses, This indicate that cultural factors significantly influence the perception and reporting.
